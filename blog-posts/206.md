# The Principle Nobody Explains Clearly (But Everyone Quotes)

You've heard it. Probably more than once. "Every cause has its effect, every effect has its cause." It shows up in Instagram captions about manifestation. It gets dropped in YouTube videos about Hermeticism like it's self-explanatory. It sounds like something you'd see on a motivational poster next to a photo of a mountain.

And because it sounds obvious, most people nod, say "yeah, that makes sense," and move right past it.

Which is a shame. Because the Principle of Cause and Effect is the most powerful of the seven Hermetic principles, and almost nobody stops long enough to ask the one question that actually matters.

If everything has a cause, and you are currently experiencing an effect, then what caused it?

Sit with that for a second. Not the bumper sticker version. The real one.

## The version everyone stops at

Here's what most people think Cause and Effect means: things happen for a reason. You eat too much sugar, you get a stomachache. You study hard, you pass the test. You punch a wall, your hand hurts. Newton's third law with a spiritual paint job.

And that's true. On that level, it's obviously true. But it's also the least interesting thing you could possibly say about this principle. If that's all it meant, the Kybalion wouldn't have bothered. The ancient Egyptians wouldn't have spent centuries developing the teaching. There would be nothing here that a physics textbook doesn't already cover.

The surface reading of Cause and Effect is so obvious that it hides what the principle is actually pointing at.

## The version that changes things

The Kybalion doesn't say "events have causes." It says the universe operates on the principle of cause and effect at every level. Every level. Not just the physical one where billiard balls bounce off each other. The mental one too.

Your thoughts are causes. Not in a vague, wishy-washy "think positive" way. In a literal, operational way that the Hermetic tradition takes seriously. Your assumptions about what's going to happen are causes. The emotional state you carry into a room is a cause. The story you've been telling yourself about who you are, that's a cause too, and it's been producing effects you've been mistaking for circumstances.

This is the claim that separates Hermeticism from every self-help framework that borrows its language: mental causation is as real as physical causation. Not a metaphor. Not a useful way to think about things. An actual mechanism. The mental plane operates by cause and effect the same way the physical plane does. What you hold in mind produces results, whether you're conscious of the process or not.

Think about what that means for a moment. If mental causation is real, then the things happening in your life right now are not random. They're not punishment. They're not luck. They are effects. And effects have causes. And if those causes are mental, then the place to look isn't out there at the circumstances. It's in here, at the assumptions.

That's uncomfortable. It should be. But uncomfortable and untrue are not the same thing.

## The Neville Goddard connection

If this sounds familiar and you've spent any time with Neville Goddard's work, there's a good reason. Neville's entire teaching is the Principle of Cause and Effect applied to assumption.

"Assume the feeling of the wish fulfilled."

That sentence is a causal instruction. You are the cause. The fulfilled wish is the effect. The gap between them is where the principle operates. Neville didn't invent this idea. He took the Hermetic framework and made it personal, made it immediate, made it something you could use while standing in line at the grocery store.

Neville understood what the Kybalion lays out: that imagination isn't escape. It's causation. When you vividly assume a state, when you enter the feeling of already being the person who has what you want, you are setting a mental cause into motion. The Hermetic tradition says the universe responds to that cause the same way it responds to any other cause. It produces an effect.

This is why Neville's techniques work for some people and not others. It's not about the visualization being pretty enough or the affirmation being repeated enough times. It's about whether you've actually established a cause. A real one. One you believe in enough that it starts generating effects. The people who get results are the ones who stop performing the exercise and start inhabiting the state. They become the cause.

The people who don't get results are still waiting for the effect to arrive from somewhere outside themselves. Which is like planting a seed and then digging it up every morning to check if it's growing.

Neville had a phrase for this gap between cause and effect: the law of assumption. He didn't call it a technique. He called it a law. Because in his framework, and in the Hermetic framework it comes from, assumption doesn't sometimes work. It always works. The question is only what you're assuming. If you assume failure, you've set that as a cause. If you assume that things work out for you, you've set that instead. Both produce effects. Every time. You just don't usually notice because the assumptions are so quiet and so old that they feel like reality rather than choices.

## The cause audit

Here's where this stops being theory and starts being useful.

Take three things currently happening in your life. Not the big dramatic stuff, the everyday things. The relationship that feels stuck. The money situation that keeps repeating. The health thing you can't seem to shake. The mood you keep waking up in.

Now trace each one back. Not to an event. Not to another person. Back to a mental cause. An assumption. A belief. A state you've been carrying around so long you forgot it was there.

Maybe the relationship feels stuck because somewhere underneath everything, you've assumed that this is just how it goes for you. That people leave. That it always ends the same way. You've been holding that assumption so steadily that it functions as a cause, and stuckness is the effect.

Maybe the money situation keeps repeating because your actual, operational belief, not the one you say out loud but the one that runs your behavior, is that there's never quite enough. That belief is a cause. And it produces the experience of there never being quite enough. Over and over.

Maybe you keep waking up in a bad mood because last thing at night, you're scrolling through things that irritate you, marinating in that state, and then sleeping on it. Eight hours of your mind processing irritation as a cause. Waking up grumpy is the effect. Of course it is.

This isn't blame. Let's be clear about that. Blame says you're bad for having these causes. This says you're powerful. There's a difference. If your mental states are producing your experience, then you're not a victim of your circumstances. You're the author of them, even if you've been writing unconsciously. That's not an accusation. It's the best news you'll get all week.

The cause audit works because it shifts your attention from the effect to the cause. And once you can see the cause, you can change it. Not easily. Not overnight. But you can change it. You could not change it before, because you didn't know it was there.

One more thing about the audit. Don't do it once and file it away. Come back to it. The first pass, you'll catch the obvious stuff. The second pass, a few days later, you'll catch things you missed because your assumptions were hiding behind other assumptions. The deep ones don't announce themselves. They just sit there, quietly producing the same effects day after day, and you've never questioned them because questioning them felt like questioning gravity.

## Why most people skip this

The reason Cause and Effect gets quoted but never explained is that the explained version asks something of you. The bumper sticker version is comfortable. Things happen for a reason. Fine. Pass the chips. The real version says: you are the reason things happen. That lands differently.

It's also why the "everything happens for a reason" crowd and the Hermetic tradition are not saying the same thing. One is passive. Things happen to you, and maybe there's a cosmic plan. The other is active. Things happen through you, and the plan is whatever you've been assuming.

Hermes didn't say the universe has a script and you're reading your lines. He said the universe is mental, and your mind is constantly writing the script. Big difference.

## The part nobody wants to hear

Here's the part that makes people uncomfortable enough to click away: if mental causation is real, then you can't keep blaming the economy, your parents, your boss, your ex, your genetics, or the weather for how your life looks. Not because those things don't matter. They do. But because they are effects too, further up the chain. And the cause you can actually work with is the one sitting behind your eyes.

This doesn't mean external circumstances don't exist. It doesn't mean bad things don't happen to people who think good thoughts. The physical plane has its own causes and effects, and sometimes a hurricane is just a hurricane. But the Hermetic claim is that There's a layer underneath the physical — the mental layer — where your assumptions and beliefs are generating the pattern that your physical life follows.

And here's what makes this genuinely useful rather than just philosophically interesting: the mental layer is the one you can change. You can't always change your circumstances directly. You can't make your boss less annoying by wishing it. You can't fix a broken car with a belief. But you can change the mental state you bring into those situations, and the Hermetic tradition says that changed state will produce changed effects. Not always immediately. Not always in the way you expected. But consistently enough that the pattern becomes impossible to ignore once you start paying attention.

You can ignore that layer. Most people do. But ignoring it doesn't stop it from operating. It just means it operates without your input.

## A place to start

Try this today. Not as a formal exercise. Just as an observation.

Pick one thing that's bothering you. Something small. The traffic that annoys you. The coworker who gets under your skin. The habit you can't break.

Instead of reacting to the effect, ask yourself: what am I assuming here? What belief do I hold about this situation that might be producing it, or at least producing my experience of it?

Don't answer too quickly. The real assumptions are the ones you don't notice because they feel like facts. They feel like "that's just how it is." Those are the causes worth finding.

You might not change anything today. That's fine. Just noticing the cause is the first step. Because once a cause is conscious, it stops running on autopilot. And causes that don't run on autopilot start producing different effects.

The Principle of Cause and Effect isn't a bumper sticker. It's an operating manual. And you've been following it whether you knew it or not. The only question is whether you want to follow it on purpose.

Most people quote it. A few people use it.

Be one of the few.
