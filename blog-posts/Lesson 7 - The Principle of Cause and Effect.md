# Lesson 7: The Principle of Cause and Effect

> *"Every Cause has its Effect; every Effect has its Cause; everything happens according to Law; Chance is but a name for Law not recognized; there are many planes of causation, but nothing escapes the Law."*
> — The Kybalion

---

## Opening Dialogue

"Master, I need to ask you something that has been bothering me since the beginning."

The Master waited.

"You teach that every cause has its effect, and every effect has its cause. That nothing happens by chance. That everything follows law. If that is true — if everything is caused — then what happens to free will? Am I not just a domino falling because the domino behind me fell?"

The Master was still for a moment, the way someone is still when they are deciding how much to say.

"Tell me," the Master said. "When you sat down this morning, did you choose to sit down, or did something make you?"

"I chose to. My legs were tired, and there was a chair."

"So your legs were the cause, and sitting was the effect?"

"No — I mean, I decided. The tiredness was part of it, but I decided."

"Good. Now tell me about a time when something happened to you — something you did not decide. Something that just arrived in your life uninvited."

The Seeker thought. "Last year. I lost a friendship that mattered to me. It just... collapsed. One day it was fine, the next it was gone. I had no say in it."

"You had no say in the ending?"

"It was their decision, not mine."

"And what were you contributing to that friendship in the months before it collapsed?"

The Seeker was quiet for a while.

"I was distracted. Busy. I had stopped calling as much. I told myself it was fine, that they understood, but I was not really present anymore."

"So the friendship did not collapse. It was withdrawn from. By you. Before they made it official."

The Seeker sat with that.

"You are not a victim of causes," the Master said. "You are a cause. Every thought you hold, every word you speak, every action you take — these are not responses. They are origins. Effects ripple outward from you constantly, whether you notice them or not. The question was never whether you have free will. The question is whether you are using it on purpose or by accident."

"What is the difference?"

"The difference is everything. The person who causes effects unconsciously — without seeing the chain of causation they are part of — lives at the mercy of consequences they never intended. They blame the world for what the world delivers, never realizing that the world is delivering what they ordered. The person who causes effects consciously — who watches their own thoughts, understands their own patterns, chooses their own mental states — that person is not a domino. That person is the hand that sets the dominos in motion."

"Then free will is not the freedom to escape cause and effect."

"No. Free will is the freedom to be a better cause."

## The Essential Revelation

The Seventh Hermetic Principle is the most demanding of the seven, because it asks you to take responsibility for something most people spend their lives avoiding: the origin of your own experience.

The Kybalion states it without apology: *Every Cause has its Effect; every Effect has its Cause; everything happens according to Law; Chance is but a name for Law not recognized.* There is no wiggle room here. Nothing is accidental. Nothing is random. Everything that exists is the effect of some prior cause, and everything you do is the cause of some future effect. The chain does not break. It does not pause. It does not make exceptions for you.

This can sound fatalistic. It is not. It is the most liberating teaching in the Hermetic tradition, because once you understand that every effect has a cause, you stop being surprised by your life and start being responsible for it.

Here is what most people miss: they spend almost all of their time on the plane of effects. Something happens — a conflict at work, a financial setback, a health scare — and they react. They manage the effect. They put out the fire. They cope. And then the next effect arrives. Life becomes an endless series of reactions to things that already happened.

The Hermetic initiate learns to work on a different plane entirely. The plane of causes.

Consider the difference. On the plane of effects, you deal with circumstances after they have formed. Your coworker is angry, and you respond to their anger. Your body is unwell, and you treat the symptoms. Your project is failing, and you scramble to save it. You are always downstream, always one step behind what is already happening.

On the plane of causes, you work upstream. Before the circumstance forms. The Hermetic teaching — and this is where it parts ways sharply with the pop-culture notion of "manifestation" — does not tell you to think happy thoughts and wait for reality to comply. It tells you something far more precise: that thoughts are causes, and that the quality of your thinking produces the quality of your experience. Not magically. Mechanically.

The Corpus Hermeticum frames this through the lens of Mind — Nous — as the generative principle of all things. In the Poimandres, Hermes learns that Mind is the architect of the cosmos. Creation begins not with matter but with thought. The entire visible world is, in a sense, an effect whose cause is mental. And you, as a being endowed with mind, participate in that causation whether you know it or not.

This is not a metaphor. The Kybalion is quite literal about it. On the mental plane, thoughts are causes. On the physical plane, circumstances are effects. The link between them is not magical — it is the natural correspondence between the planes of existence. As above, so below. As within, so without. The state of your inner world shapes the conditions of your outer world. Not instantly. Not perfectly. But reliably.

A word of caution: this does not mean every bad thing that happens to you is your fault. The chain of causation is vast. Other people have their own causes producing effects that collide with yours. Collective causes — cultural, economic, biological — produce effects no single individual chose. You did not cause the storm. You did not cause someone else's cruelty.

But here is what you can cause: your response. Your mental state. The quality of attention you bring to what happens to you. That mental state becomes a cause of its own, producing the next round of effects. You cannot control the chain of causation that runs through the entire world. You can learn to control the chain that runs through you.

The Kybalion notes there are *many planes of causation*. Causes operate on the physical plane — you touch a hot stove, you get burned. On the mental plane — you hold a belief for years, and it shapes every decision without your noticing. On the spiritual plane — your deepest orientation of consciousness determines which thoughts arise.

Most people only see physical causation. They understand that actions have consequences. What they miss is that actions are themselves effects — downstream from thoughts, beliefs, attitudes, and mental states that were set in motion long before the action was taken. If you want to change your effects, you must go further upstream. You must change your causes. And your causes, at the deepest level, are mental.

This is the art. Not wishing for different circumstances. Not positive thinking as a bandage over negative patterns. The art is learning to observe your own mental causation — to see the thoughts you are holding, the beliefs you are running, the emotional states you are perpetuating — and to choose, deliberately, which causes you want to set in motion.

The Emerald Tablet describes transformation as a process of ascent and descent: *It ascends from the earth to the heaven, and again it descends to the earth, and receives the force of things superior and inferior.* The alchemist does not stay on one plane. They learn to move between them — to rise to the mental plane where causes originate, and to descend to the physical plane where effects manifest. The mastery is in the movement. See the cause. Understand the effect. Choose the next cause. This is the work.

You have spent six lessons learning the principles — mentalism, correspondence, vibration, polarity, rhythm, and now cause and effect. Each one gave you a lens for understanding how reality operates. This final principle ties them together: you are not merely subject to these laws. You participate in them. You are a cause. The question is whether you are a conscious one.

## Sacred Contemplation

*Three passages. Read them in order. Notice how the same truth moves through different traditions like water through different vessels — same substance, different shape.*

**From the Corpus Hermeticum (Book XII):**

*"For Mind, the Father of all, He who is Life and Light, gave birth to Man, a being like unto Himself. And Man, having received the portion of the Father's mind, looked upon the creation of the brother who had charge of the fire, and loved it, and wished to dwell in it. And having the full authority, he descended into the generation of the cosmos and broke through the circumference of the spheres, showing to the lower nature the beautiful form of God."*

The mind you carry is a fragment of the generating Mind. When you think, you create. When you create, you cause. This is your inheritance.

**From the Emerald Tablet:**

*"Thou shalt separate the Earth from the Fire, the subtle from the gross, gently, with great industry. It ascends from the Earth to the Heaven, and again it descends to the Earth, and receives the force of things superior and inferior."*

Separation — the alchemist's first work — is the act of distinguishing cause from effect, subtle from gross. You cannot work with causation until you can see which is which.

**From the Kybalion:**

*"Every thought we think, every act we perform, has its direct and indirect results which fit into the great chain of Cause and Effect."*

Not some thoughts. Not important acts. Every thought. Every act. The chain does not distinguish between what you do on purpose and what you do carelessly. It responds to all of it.

**Reflection:** Three traditions, one teaching. The Hermeticum tells you that your mind participates in creation. The Emerald Tablet tells you the work begins with seeing clearly — separating what causes from what is caused. The Kybalion tells you that nothing escapes the law. You are already causing. The only question is whether you will do it with your eyes open.

## The Alchemical Working

This exercise is called Cause Tracing. It takes ten to fifteen minutes and asks you to do something most people never do: follow an effect backward to its source inside your own mind.

**Step One: Choose your effect.** Pick one situation in your life right now that frustrates you. Not a minor annoyance — something real. A relationship that feels stuck. A pattern you cannot seem to break. A result you keep getting that you do not want. Name it clearly. Write it down in one or two sentences.

**Step Two: Trace backward.** Now, instead of reacting to the situation — instead of analyzing what someone else did wrong, or what the world owes you, or how unfair it is — ask yourself one question: *What was I thinking before this showed up?*

Not yesterday. Not this morning. In the weeks and months before this situation took its current shape, what was the mental weather? What thoughts were you holding? What beliefs were running in the background? What emotional state were you living in — not occasionally, but habitually?

Write what comes up. Do not judge it. Do not defend it. Just trace.

**Step Three: Go deeper.** Now ask: *And before that thought, what belief made it feel true?* Every recurring thought rests on a deeper assumption. "Nothing I do matters" rests on a belief about your own powerlessness. "People always leave" rests on a belief about your worthiness of connection. "I can never get ahead" rests on a belief about the nature of the world and your place in it. Find the belief. Write it down.

**Step Four: Name the cause.** By now you should be looking at something specific — a mental pattern, a habitual thought, a deep belief — that has been operating as a cause in your life. You may not have chosen it consciously. It may have been there so long you forgot it was a choice. But it is a cause, and the situation you named in Step One is its effect.

**Step Five: Ask the liberating question.** Do not try to force a new belief. Do not affirm the opposite. Just ask: *What thought could I hold instead?* Not a thought that denies reality. Not a thought that pretends the situation does not exist. A thought that opens a different causal chain. A thought that, if held consistently, would begin to produce different effects over time.

Write it down. One sentence. Something you can actually believe, not something you wish were true.

That sentence is your new cause. You do not need to act on it today. You need to hold it. To return to it. To let it begin its work.

## Living Application

Cause and effect is not a theory. It is the machinery of your daily life, whether you have noticed it or not.

**In relationships.** Your emotional state is contagious. Not in a vague, mystical sense — in a practical, observable one. When you walk into a room carrying resentment, the people in that room respond to it. They may not know what they are responding to. They may not be able to name it. But they feel it, and they adjust. Your tension becomes their tension. Your openness becomes their openness. This is not manipulation. It is correspondence. The emotional cause you carry produces an emotional effect in your environment. If your relationships keep hitting the same wall, the cause is not always on the other side of the room.

**In work.** Attitude precedes results. This is not motivational rhetoric — it is a description of how mental causation operates. The person who approaches a task with conviction thinks differently than the person who approaches it with dread. Not because conviction makes the task easier, but because the quality of attention you bring determines which options you see, which risks you take, which connections you make. Two people with identical skills and identical circumstances will produce different outcomes if their mental causes are different. This is not magical thinking. It is the ordinary, observable fact that how you think changes what you do.

**In health.** The body is downstream of the mind. Chronic anxiety produces tension, which produces pain. Persistent anger produces inflammation. Habitual stress produces cortisol, which produces physical effects no medication can fully address if the mental cause remains untouched. This does not mean you can think your way out of illness. It means mental patterns are part of the causal chain, and ignoring them while treating only the physical effect is like mopping the floor while the faucet runs.

The deeper point: mental causation is not about controlling the world through thought. It is about understanding that you are already shaping your experience through thought, and that learning to do so deliberately — rather than by habit or accident — is one of the most practical skills you can develop.

**Micro-habits for daily integration:**

- When something goes wrong, pause and ask: *What was I carrying before this happened?* Not to blame yourself. To see the chain.
- Once a day, notice one thought you have been thinking on repeat. Name it. See it as a cause you have been setting in motion.
- Before any important conversation, check your mental state. What are you bringing into this? If you do not like the answer, change the state before you proceed.
- When you catch yourself saying "this always happens to me," stop. That sentence is a cause training your mind to produce the pattern it describes.

You do not need to master this in a day. You need to start seeing it. Once you see the chain of causation running through your own life, you cannot unsee it. And that seeing is the beginning of everything that follows.

## The Soul's Reflection

*These questions are for your journal and nowhere else. Answer them for yourself. The paper does not judge.*

1. Name one recurring pattern in your life — something that keeps showing up in different forms. What mental cause might be producing this effect? Be honest about your own role in the chain.

2. When something goes wrong, where do you usually look first — outward at circumstances, or inward at your own contribution? What would change if you reversed that habit?

3. What is one belief you hold about yourself that you did not choose? Where did it come from? What effects has it produced in your life?

4. Think of a time when your mental state clearly produced a visible effect — when your attitude changed an outcome, when your mood altered a room, when your thinking shifted a result. What did that teach you about your own causation?

5. If you are honest, are you currently living more on the plane of effects — reacting to what happens — or on the plane of causes — shaping what happens? What would it take to move upstream?

6. What is one thought you could hold today that would begin to produce a different effect in your life? Not a grand thought. A true one.

7. You have now encountered all seven Hermetic principles. Which one landed hardest? Which one are you still working to understand? What has shifted in you since you began?

## The Initiate's Apprenticeship

You have reached the final practice of Book One. This one asks you to do what the entire series has been building toward: observe yourself with clear eyes.

**The practice:** Each evening this week, trace one effect from your day back to its mental cause. Do not fix anything. Do not judge anything. Just trace the chain.

Pick one thing that happened today — something that went well, something that went poorly, something that surprised you, something that frustrated you. Write it down as the effect. Then ask three questions:

1. *What was I thinking before this happened?*
2. *What was my emotional state?*
3. *What belief or habit of mind might have set this in motion?*

Write your answers. One sentence each is enough. Then put it down and go to sleep.

After seven days, read your log from beginning to end. Look for patterns. You will find them. There will be a thought that keeps appearing — a mental state you default to, a belief that runs underneath many of your effects. Name it. That is your primary cause. The one you have been setting in motion without knowing it.

**What to watch for:**

- Surprise. You will see connections you have never seen before. Effects you blamed on circumstances will reveal their roots in your own thinking. This is not guilt. It is power.
- Resistance. Some of what you find will be uncomfortable. You may want to defend the thought patterns that produced effects you did not want. Notice that resistance.
- Clarity. By day four or five, something usually clicks. You start seeing causation in real time — catching yourself mid-thought, recognizing the cause before the effect has even formed. That is the beginning of the work.

Seven days. One effect per evening. Three questions. The chain reveals itself to those who look.
