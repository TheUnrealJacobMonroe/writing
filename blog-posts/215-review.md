## Detailed Findings

### Hermetic Accuracy — One Flag

The post correctly identifies:
- Marsilio Ficino as translator of the Corpus Hermeticum (~1463)
- Cosimo de' Medici as the patron who commissioned it
- Ficino as a Neoplatonist
- The fall of Constantinople (1453) as the catalyst for manuscripts arriving in Italy
- The influence chain: Ficino → Bruno, Dee, Newton → Theosophy → Kybalion

**Questionable claim:** "Cosimo had assigned the job to a scholar named Gemistus Pletho, a Neoplatonist from Constantinople who had been living in Florence and stirring up philosophical controversy. But Pletho died before he could finish."

Pletho (d. 1452/1454) did visit Florence in 1438–39 and inspired Cosimo's interest in Greek philosophy, but the standard historical account is that Cosimo directly commissioned *Ficino* to translate the newly acquired Hermetic manuscripts in 1463. The story about Pletho being the original translator assigned to the Hermetic texts specifically is not well-supported. Pletho's influence was more about Plato than Hermes. This needs fact-checking or removal.

### Anti-AI Scan

**Em dashes:** 0. Clean. ✅

**AI vocabulary:** Light usage detected. The post mostly avoids the kill list.

**Negative parallelisms (AI pattern #9):** 4 instances — over threshold. These read as a recognizable LLM tic:

1. "It wasn't just foreign words to him; it was a philosophical architecture he recognized."
2. "He wasn't trying to save Hermeticism. He was doing his job."
3. "It's not about changing the physical composition of metals. It's about taking raw material and transmuting it into something of higher order."
4. "The Great Work isn't about X. It's about Y."

Recommend cutting to 1–2 max. Convert the others to plain declarative statements.

**Rule of three (AI pattern #10):** Present in 2 places:

1. "Someone has to write it down, translate it, publish it, teach it. Someone has to care enough to carry it from one generation to the next." — This is a list of four, not three, and it reads naturally. Acceptable.
2. "Monks copied it, because scholars fled with it, because Ficino translated it, because the printing press made it available, because thinkers read it and built on it." — Five-item chain. Reads fine, arguably natural.

Low concern here. These work rhythmically.

**Inflated symbolism (humanizer #1):** One instance:

- "had once been the intellectual oxygen of the ancient Mediterranean world" — "intellectual oxygen" is a forced metaphor. Replace with something more grounded.

**"Not just X, but Y" / "It's not X, it's Y" constructions:** Present 4+ times. While not all are strictly AI-pattern negative parallelisms, the frequency creates a repetitive rhetorical rhythm that signals LLM authorship. Suggest varying at least 2 of them.

The "Here's what gets me about his story" line is a mild first-person editorial usage — borderline but acceptable given the style guide allows philosophical/editorial "I."

### Structure

The "Why This Matters" section reads slightly summary-like but pulls back with the Hermetic reflection, which saves it.

2. **Negative parallelisms** — 4 instances exceed the spirit of the anti-AI rules. Need to reduce to ≤2.

**Non-blocking suggestions:**
- Replace "intellectual oxygen" with a more natural phrase
- Trim one or two "not X, it's Y" constructions
- Consider whether "Here's what gets me" editorial voice is intentional or accidental

**After fixes:** This post is very close to a clean PASS. The narrative is engaging, the word count is solid, the tone matches the style guide, and the historical content is mostly accurate. Just needs a Pletho fact-check and a quick negative-parallelism trim.
