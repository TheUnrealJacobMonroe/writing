# Why Forgiveness Is Not What You Think It Is

Someone hurt you. Maybe recently, maybe years ago. And every piece of advice you have received says the same thing: forgive them. Let it go. Move on. You have tried. You said the words. You did the journaling exercise. You even prayed about it. And the anger is still there. The resentment is still there. The hurt is still there.

You think you are failing at forgiveness.

You are not failing. You are doing it wrong. Forgiveness is not what the internet told you it is.

## The Forgiveness You Were Sold

Here is what most people mean when they say "forgive." They mean one of three things, and none of them are forgiveness.

The first version is condoning. Someone wrongs you, and forgiving means acting like it was okay. "They didn't mean it." "They were going through a hard time." You swallow what happened, paste a serene expression on your face, and pretend the wound does not exist. This is not forgiveness. This is betrayal dressed in spiritual clothing. You are telling yourself that what happened to you does not matter, and your body knows you are lying.

The second version is reconciling. Forgiveness means letting the person back in. Continuing the relationship. Acting normal at family dinners. Everyone acts like the thing that happened is in the past because you "forgave." But the past is not behind you if you are still flinching every time they raise their voice. Reconciliation is an external act. It requires another person. Forgiveness does not.

The third version is suppression. You push it down. You "choose" to forgive the way you choose to eat a salad when you want pizza. It is a discipline exercise, not a genuine shift. You stuff the resentment into a drawer and hope it stays there. It never stays there. It comes out sideways: in sarcasm, in tension, in the way you describe the person to your friends, in the fact that you still think about it at 2 AM.

None of this is forgiveness. And the reason it keeps failing is not because you are weak or unspiritual or not trying hard enough. It is because you are trying to jump across a gap that cannot be jumped. You are trying to teleport from resentment to peace, and that is not how this works.

## The Polarity Problem

There is an old Hermetic teaching, recorded in the Kybalion, that describes how reality is structured around pairs of opposites. Hot and cold. Light and dark. Love and hate. The teaching says these pairs are not separate things. They are the same thing, experienced at different degrees. Hot and cold are both temperature. Love and hate are both emotional intensity directed at someone. They share an axis.

This is the Principle of Polarity, and it changes everything about how forgiveness actually works.

Resentment and forgiveness sit on the same axis. They are not enemies. They are poles. And here is the part that matters: you cannot jump from one pole to the other. You can only slide along the axis.

Think of it like a dimmer switch. You cannot go from pitch black to full brightness in one instant. There is a gradient. There are degrees in between. Full resentment is one end. Full forgiveness is the other. And between them is a whole spectrum of movement. Small shifts. Incremental changes. Gradual release.

What most people try to do is flip the switch from off to on. They try to go from "I am furious at this person" to "I have fully forgiven them" in a single leap. And when that leap fails, which it always does, they conclude that forgiveness is impossible, or that they are not good enough to achieve it.

The problem is not you. The problem is the teleportation fantasy. You are trying to do something that violates the structure of reality itself. Polarity does not work that way. Nothing works that way.

## Forgiveness Is Not About the Other Person

Here is the reframe that changes everything.

Forgiveness is not about the person who hurt you. It is not about what they did, whether they are sorry, whether they deserve it, or whether they have changed. Forgiveness is about the grip that the event has on your internal state. It is about you, not them.

Think about what actually happens when you hold a resentment. Someone wronged you ten years ago, and every time you think about it, your jaw tightens. Your chest gets heavy. Your mood drops. You replay the scene. You argue with the imaginary version of them. You win the argument. You still feel terrible.

That person is not doing that to you right now. You are doing it to yourself. Not because you are choosing to suffer — nobody chooses that — but because the event left a charge in your system, and that charge keeps firing. The resentment is not a story about them. It is an energetic state inside you that has not been released.

Forgiveness is the process of releasing that internal grip. Not the external one. You do not have to talk to the person. You do not have to see them. You do not have to reconcile, or make peace, or pretend anything. You do not even have to tell them. Forgiveness is an internal act of releasing the hold that the memory has on your present-moment state.

That is it. That is what forgiveness actually is. And once you understand this, the whole thing gets simpler. Not easier. But simpler.

## The Micro-Step Protocol

So how do you actually do it? Here is a Polarity-based protocol that works because it respects the axis. It does not ask you to jump. It asks you to slide.

**Step 1: Name the emotion.**

Not "I'm angry." That is too vague. Get specific. What is the precise thing you feel when you think about this person or this event? Is it resentment? Betrayal? Humiliation? Grief? Name it. The act of naming is itself a shift. It moves the emotion from the background noise of your psyche into the light where you can see it.

Sit with it for a moment. Do not try to fix it. Just name it clearly. "I feel resentment toward this person because they humiliated me in front of people I respected." That level of specificity matters.

**Step 2: Identify the axis.**

Now ask: what is the opposite of this specific feeling? Not "forgiveness" in the abstract. The specific opposite. If the feeling is resentment, maybe the opposite is indifference. Not love, not warmth, just the absence of charge. If the feeling is betrayal, maybe the opposite is trust. Not in this person, but in your own ability to navigate what happened. If the feeling is humiliation, maybe the opposite is dignity. A quiet, unshakable sense that what happened does not define you.

You are mapping the axis. You are naming both poles so you know which direction to move.

**Step 3: Take one micro-step.**

Do not try to reach the opposite pole. That is the mistake everyone makes. Instead, ask yourself: what is one small movement I can make toward the other end of this axis today?

Not forgiveness. One step closer to forgiveness.

Maybe it is: "I acknowledge that this happened and I cannot change it." Maybe it is: "I choose not to replay this scene today." Maybe it is: "I am going to stop telling this story to my friends for one week." Maybe it is as small as: "When this memory comes up, I will notice it without following it."

The micro-step is not a resolution. It is a vibration shift. You are changing your internal state by one degree. And one degree matters, because this is a polarity axis, not a binary switch. Every degree of movement is real movement.

**Step 4: Repeat daily.**

This is the part nobody wants to hear. Forgiveness is not a one-time event. It is a daily practice of sliding along the axis, one micro-step at a time. Some days you will move further. Some days you will barely move at all. Some days you will slide backward, and that is fine. The pendulum swings, and rhythm is part of the process.

But over days and weeks, something shifts. The charge gets weaker. The memory comes up less often. When it does, the grip is looser. You find yourself thinking about it and feeling... less. Not nothing. Not yet. But less. And "less" is movement. "Less" is the axis working.

Eventually, and this might take months for the deep stuff, you arrive at a place where the event still happened, but it no longer owns your internal state. You can think about it without spiraling. You can hear the person's name without flinching. That is not suppression. That is genuine release. That is what forgiveness actually feels like.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Let's be honest about a few things.

First, some things are extraordinarily hard to forgive. Betrayal by someone you trusted completely. Abuse. The kind of wounding that reshapes how you see the world. This protocol works for those things, but it works slowly. Anyone who tells you to forgive deep trauma quickly is selling something. The micro-step approach respects the depth of the wound by not pretending it is smaller than it is.

Second, forgiveness is not reconciliation. This cannot be said loudly enough. You can forgive someone fully and completely and still choose never to see them again. You can forgive someone and maintain every boundary you have set. Forgiveness is an internal act of release. Reconciliation is an external act of reconnection. They are independent. One does not require the other.

If someone tells you that you have not really forgiven someone because you will not let them back in your life, they do not understand what forgiveness is. You are allowed to release the charge and keep the boundary. In fact, that is often the healthiest thing you can do.

Third, the Kybalion's teaching on Polarity is not just about forgiveness. It is about everything. Every emotional state you experience sits on an axis, and you can always move along it. You are never stuck at a pole. You might be far from where you want to be, but movement is always available. One degree is always available.

That is the real gift of this principle. Not that it makes forgiveness easy. But that it makes forgiveness possible. When you stop trying to jump and start trying to slide, the whole process opens up. It stops being a test of your character and starts being a practice of gradual, honest, incremental release.

## Start Here

Think of one resentment you have been carrying. One that still has a charge. You know which one. It is the one that came to mind when you started reading this.

Now do not try to forgive it. Just name it. Get specific about the emotion. Identify what the opposite of that feeling would be, for you, in this situation. And then ask yourself one question: what is the smallest possible movement I could make toward that opposite today?

Not a big movement. Not a resolution. Not a declaration. One micro-step. One degree along the axis.

Do that today. Do it again tomorrow. And the day after. Let the polarity do what polarity does. Let the shift build.

That is forgiveness. Not the version you were sold. The real one.
